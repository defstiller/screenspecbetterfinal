Project example

Composite roof enclosure with riser wall

This project is a good example of what happens when a standard screen roof is not enough and the enclosure needs a more finished roof line, more shade, and a cleaner visual tie-in back to the home.

The riser wall matters here because it changes how the roof line feels and how the enclosure sits against the house.
Why it matters

On a roofed enclosure like this, the shape of the roof and the way it finishes against the house do as much work visually as the screened walls themselves.

Project type Covered enclosure with a composite roof and a riser wall for a stronger finished roof line.
Best fit for Patios and outdoor spaces that need more shade and a more finished covered feel than a screen roof alone can provide.
Related pages Composite and pan roof enclosures and Ponte Vedra projects.
What this example shows

Why someone would choose this type of roofed enclosure

More shade and weather coverage

A covered roof system changes the space more dramatically than a standard screen roof when comfort and coverage are the priorities.

A stronger finished look

The riser wall helps the enclosure feel less temporary and more like part of the home instead of a lighter add-on.

A different planning conversation

Roofed projects usually need more discussion around roof type, tie-in, and how much coverage you want than a standard screened build.

Good questions to ask on a project like this
  • Do you want full shade and rain coverage or a lighter screen roof feel?
  • Is a composite roof the right answer, or is a pan roof enough for the space?
  • Does the enclosure need a riser wall to make the roof line finish correctly?
  • Is the project mostly a covered patio conversion or part of a larger new enclosure plan?
Where this fits

Usually a better fit for the roof-systems page

This kind of job belongs on the composite and pan roof page when the roof is doing most of the work in the project. It is usually not the same conversation as a standard screen-only build.
